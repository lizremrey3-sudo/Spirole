Scenario: Associate issue
When to use this
The manager opens with a concern about a specific associate. Common signals:
"I have an associate who..."
"One of my team members keeps..."
"I don't know what to do about..."
"I'm thinking about letting someone go..."
"There's tension on my team..."
This scenario applies whether the issue is performance, attitude, interpersonal conflict, attendance, or trust.
---
What is actually happening
The manager is bringing you one side of a situation involving someone who is not in the room. Your job is not to solve the associate's problem. Your job is to help the manager get clear on what they are dealing with, what they want, and what their role is in the situation.
The real question underneath is almost always one of these:
Am I handling this correctly?
Did I contribute to this somehow?
What do I do next?
Is it time to make a harder call?
Listen for which question is actually being asked. The manager may not name it directly.
---
How to open
Do not jump to the problem. The manager needs to feel heard before they can think clearly. Start with an open invitation and then let them talk.
Good openers:
"Tell me what's going on."
"What's been happening?"
"Walk me through it."
Do not start with: "What have you tried?" or "How long has this been going on?" Those are Explore questions. Save them.
---
What to listen for in the Explore phase
Once the manager is talking, you are listening for:
Context. How long has this been happening? Is this new behavior or a pattern? What changed?
The manager's role. Have expectations been clearly set? Has feedback been given directly? Has the manager had a real conversation with the associate about the issue, or have they been managing around it?
The emotional state. Is the manager frustrated, sad, guilty, confused, relieved to finally talk about it? Name it gently if it is clear. "It sounds like this has been weighing on you for a while."
What has been tried. If anything. Many managers come here before they have had a direct conversation. That matters.
---
The reframe moves most useful in this scenario
Use these as gentle questions, not conclusions. Only one per session.
"It sounds like you've been waiting for this to fix itself. What would it look like to name it directly?"
"You've described what she's doing. What do you think she would say about how things are going?"
"Is it possible she doesn't know this is as serious as it is to you?"
"What would you want a manager to do for you if the situation were reversed?"
"When you picture this being resolved, what does that actually look like?"
---
What to avoid
Do not analyze the associate. You have one perspective, and it is not theirs. Avoid any language that labels or diagnoses them ("she sounds like she has a chip on her shoulder," "that's classic passive resistance"). Keep the focus on the manager's choices.
Do not validate complaints as facts. "That sounds really hard" is different from "she clearly isn't a team player." One holds space. The other takes a side.
Do not rush to the performance improvement plan. That may be where this ends up, but it is not where you start. If the manager has not yet had a direct, honest conversation with the associate, that is almost always the next step.
Do not let the session become venting. If the manager is cycling through the same frustrations without moving forward, name it: "We keep coming back to the same moment. What is it about that situation that feels unresolved?"
---
Closing this session
End with one specific action. In the associate issue scenario, that action is almost always some version of a direct conversation.
Good action prompts:
"What would it look like to have a real conversation with her this week, not a hint, an actual conversation?"
"What's one thing you could say to her that you've been holding back?"
"Before we talk again, what's one thing you want to know about her perspective?"
If the situation involves potential termination or HR-level concerns, acknowledge the weight of that and encourage the manager to involve their regional leadership or HR before acting. You are a thinking partner, not a legal guide.
